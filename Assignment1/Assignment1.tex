\section{The Pseudocode for Lab2A}

The pseudocode of the Lab2A is shown as Algorithm \ref{alg:GSSC}.
\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Gale-Shapley Algorithm for Student-College Matching}
\label{alg:GSSC}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Number of students $N$, number of colleges $M$, student preferences $s$, college capacities $c$
\Ensure Stable matching between students and colleges
\State Initialize $nxt[0 \dots N-1] \gets 0$ \Comment{Next college to apply for each student}
\State Initialize $college\_heaps[0 \dots M-1]$ as min-heaps \Comment{Store admitted (score, student) pairs}
\State Initialize queue $free\_students \gets \{0, 1, \dots, N-1\}$
\While{$free\_students$ is not empty}
    \State $stu \gets free\_students.pop()$
    \If{$nxt[stu] \geq |s[stu].pref|$}
        \State \textbf{continue} \Comment{No more colleges to apply}
    \EndIf
    \State $col \gets s[stu].pref[nxt[stu]] - 1$
 
    \State $nxt[stu] \gets nxt[stu] + 1$
    \State $score \gets c[col].comment[stu]$
    \If{$college\_heaps[col].size < c[col].cap$}
        \If{$score > 0$}
            \State $college\_heaps[col].push((score, stu))$
        \Else
            \State $free\_students.push(stu)$
        \EndIf
    \ElsIf{$college\_heaps[col]$ is not empty}
        \State $(worst\_score, worst\_stu) \gets college\_heaps[col].top()$
       
        \If{$score > worst\_score$}
            \State $college\_heaps[col].pop()$
            \State $college\_heaps[col].push((score, stu))$
            \State $free\_students.push(worst\_stu)$
        \Else
            \State $free\_students.push(stu)$
        \EndIf
    \Else
        \State $free\_students.push(stu)$
    \EndIf
\EndWhile
\For{$i = 0$ to $M-1$}
    \State Extract all students from $college\_heaps[i]$ and output
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Problem 1: The Complexity Analysis}

The sorting of the students' preferences takes $O(NM \log M)$ time. 

Each student will apply to each college at most once in this algorithm. However, since we use min-heaps to maintain the admitted students for each college, each insertion or deletion takes $O(\log C)$ time, where $C$ is the maximum capacity of a college. 

The output step takes $O(MC \log C)$ time to extract and sort the admitted students for each college, which is dominated by the main loop. Thus, the overall time complexity of the algorithm is $O(NM \log M + NM \log C)$, which simplifies to $O(NM \log M)$ as the college capacity $C$ is typically much smaller than the number of students $N$.

For $N$ students, we use an array with length $M$ to store the satisfaction score, which is the dominant term. We also use a queue ($O(N)$) and $M$ priority queues (heaps) in the process ($O(MN)$). Thus the space complexity will be $O(MN)$.

\section{Problem 2: Stability of the Matching}

\begin{definition}[Stable Matching for Student-College Setting]
    A matching is \textbf{stable} if it satisfies the following conditions:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Individual Rationality:} No student is matched to a college with non-positive satisfaction score, and no college admits a student with non-positive score.
        \item \textbf{No Blocking Pair:} There does not exist a pair $(s, c)$ such that:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Student $s$ prefers college $c$ to his/her current matched college (or $s$ is unmatched but $c$ gives positive satisfaction to $s$);
            \item College $c$ prefers student $s$ to at least one of its currently admitted students (or $c$ has remaining capacity and $s$ gives positive satisfaction to $c$).
        \end{itemize}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Assume all preferences are strict (no ties), and a pair $(s,c)$ is acceptable only when both scores are positive. We prove stability by showing that no blocking pair can exist.

\begin{lemma}[Rejection Invariant]
If a student $s$ is rejected by a college $c$ at some time, then from that moment on, college $c$ always keeps a full set of students that are all strictly preferred to $s$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
At the rejection moment, $c$ is full and $s$ is no better than the current worst admitted student, so every admitted student at $c$ is strictly preferred to $s$. Later, the algorithm can only replace an admitted student of $c$ by another student that $c$ prefers more (because replacement occurs only when the new score is larger than the current minimum). Therefore, after the rejection, every student kept by $c$ remains strictly preferred to $s$ forever.
\end{proof}

Now suppose, for contradiction, that the final matching has a blocking pair $(s,c)$. Then $s$ prefers $c$ to $\,\mu(s)$ (or $s$ is unmatched) and $c$ prefers $s$ to some final admitted student (or has free capacity).

Because $s$ prefers $c$ to the final outcome, student $s$ must have applied to $c$ before reaching $\mu(s)$ (or before becoming unmatched). Since $(s,c)$ is not in the output, $s$ was rejected by $c$ at some point. By the rejection invariant, after that rejection, college $c$ always keeps a full set of students all preferred to $s$. Hence in the final matching, $c$ cannot have free capacity and cannot prefer $s$ to any admitted student. This contradicts that $(s,c)$ is a blocking pair.

Therefore, no blocking pair exists, and the produced matching is stable.

\section{Problem 3: Student's Optimality}

\begin{definition}[Student-Optimal Matching]
    A stable matching is \textbf{student-optimal} if every student is matched to the best college that he/she can possibly obtain in any stable matching. Formally, for each student $s$, let $\mu(s)$ be the college matched to $s$ in the student-optimal matching, and $\mu'(s)$ be the college matched to $s$ in any other stable matching. Then $w(s, \mu(s)) \geq w(s, \mu'(s))$ for all students $s$.
\end{definition}

We prove that the student-applying GS algorithm produces the student-optimal stable matching.

\begin{lemma}
    If a student $s$ is rejected by a college $c$ during the algorithm, then no stable matching can pair $s$ with $c$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    Suppose for contradiction that there exists a stable matching $\mu^*$ and a rejected pair $(s,c)$ such that $(s,c) \in \mu^*$. Among all such counterexamples, choose one where the rejection of $s$ by $c$ happens earliest in the algorithm.

    At that rejection moment, college $c$ is full with students $d_1,\ldots,d_k$, each strictly preferred by $c$ to $s$. Since $\mu^*$ matches $s$ with $c$, at least one of these students, say $d$, is not matched to $c$ in $\mu^*$.

    By the choice of earliest counterexample, before this moment student $d$ has not been rejected by the college assigned to $d$ in $\mu^*$ (otherwise we get an earlier rejected pair that also appears in $\mu^*$). Therefore, when $d$ is currently at $c$, student $d$ has not yet failed at $\mu^*(d)$, so $d$ weakly prefers $c$ to $\mu^*(d)$; with strict preferences and different colleges, this means $d$ strictly prefers $c$ to $\mu^*(d)$ (or to being unmatched).

    College $c$ strictly prefers $d$ to $s$ by construction at the rejection moment. Hence $(d,c)$ is a blocking pair of $\mu^*$, contradicting the stability of $\mu^*$.

    So no stable matching can contain any rejected pair.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Main Proof:} We prove that the algorithm produces the student-optimal matching. Consider any student $s$ who is matched to college $\mu(s)$ in the algorithm output. If $s$ is unmatched, then $s$ was rejected by all colleges with positive satisfaction, and by the Lemma, no stable matching can pair $s$ with any acceptable college.

If $s$ is matched to $\mu(s) = c$, suppose for contradiction that there exists a stable matching $\mu'$ where $s$ is matched to $c'$ with $w(s, c') > w(s, c)$. Since $s$ prefers $c'$ to $c$, student $s$ applied to $c'$ before $c$ in the algorithm. For $s$ to end up at $c$, $s$ must have been rejected by $c'$ at some point. By the Lemma, no stable matching can pair $s$ with $c'$, contradicting that $\mu'$ is stable with $(s, c') \in \mu'$.

Therefore, no student can obtain a better college in any stable matching than what they obtain in the algorithm output. This proves that the student-applying GS algorithm produces the student-optimal stable matching.
